Tetris is Turing-complete

An Excursion from Tetris to NAND and Back Again

This article presents a method for embedding a programmable, general-purpose, digital computer into Tetris. The capabilities and performance of an implementation are demonstrated by running Tetris on Tetris.

Preliminaries

The playfield is the grid of square cells where Tetris is played. The standard playfield has 20 visible rows and 10 columns, but the dimensions are arbitrary.

Each playfield cell is in one of two states: empty or solid.

Tetriminos—the falling pieces—consist four solid cells painted any color. The seven Tetriminos have single-character, shape-based names.

A rotation system describes how Tetriminos rotate in relation to the surrounding cells and the playfield boundaries. The method employs the right-handed Nintendo Rotation System (NRS) due to its simplicity, but various rotation systems exist and any of them could be adapted.

NRS centers each Tetrimino in a 5×5 matrix and it rotates them 90° about that center. Of the 28 possible orientations, it uses only 19.

The left-most column comprises the spawn orientations, the way the pieces initially appear. The steps of clockwise rotation span left-to-right.

The mnemonics above each orientation consist of the piece type concatenated with the first letter of “down”, “left”, “up”, “right”, “horizontal”, “vertical”, or nothing for O.

A move is a 90° rotation or a 1-cell translation of the piece in play. The following terms apply to translation.

  • A shift is a horizontal translation.
  • A drop is a downward translation.
    • A soft drop is a player-controlled, 1-cell drop.
    • A hard drop is a meta-move that forces the piece as far down as it can go and locks it into place. It is operationally-equivalent to repeated soft drops.
    • A gravity drop is an automatic, 1-cell drop triggered by the fall timer.

A move is preceded by a request to move. Per NRS, the request is granted iff all four cells of the transformed Tetrimino are empty and they are within the bounds of the playfield. If the fall timer’s request for a gravity drop is denied, the piece locks and the next piece spawns.

Spawning involves a randomizer, the mechanism that determines the sequence of pieces presented to the player. Any randomizer guaranteed to eventually produce all seven Tetriminos from any point in the sequence will work with the method.

Method Overview

On an infinite playfield—bounded only by the floor—an input data set is encoded into the cells of the bottom-two rows. Tetriminos are dropped onto those cells, forming a structure logically-equivalent to a Boolean circuit. From the top-two rows of that structure, an output data set is decoded.

The output rows serve as input rows of a structure built above it and the process can be repeated indefinitely. The effect is a state machine where the data rows are states and the structures are transition functions.

A construction exists where the data capacity of a state always exceeds its predecessor. It is a means of building an abstract computing device with unbounded storage, a.k.a. a Turing machine.

The Blindfolded Player

Tetris requires a player. But if the player can see the playfield, then constructing a Turing machine is trivial. A sighted player can read input data, perform calculations via any mechanism, and drop pieces to write output data. In other words, it can make Tetris function like RAM.

To inhibit the player from contributing to calculations, it must play blindfolded. Input data, intermediate states, and output data must be inaccessible to the player. With that constraint, if the system composed of the player and the game processes information in the manor of a computer, then Tetris must be functioning as both RAM and a CPU.

Infinite Playfield

On an infinite playfield, completing a line requires infinite pieces and infinite time, neither of which the player can provide. Instead, the player deliberately grows the pile, building unbounded yet finite structures. And because lines are never cleared, the game always remains on the starting level (at the slowest drop speed).

Tetriminos spawn in column 0 at infinite height. Due to the nature of infinity, as a spawned piece falls, it never gets any closer to the floor regardless of the drop speed. In finite (though potentially vast) time, the player can shift the piece into any column. And once in position, it can hard drop to lock the piece into the surface of the pile.

The player can also perform a semihard drop. It works like a hard drop, but instead of stopping when the piece can fall no further, it stops at a specified height. It is the only way to translate a piece from infinity to a finite distance above the floor.

Spawn Signal

Since the player cannot see, the game signals when a spawn occurs. It reveals the randomly selected piece to the player, who responds with a timed sequence of requests to move it. If the playfield were empty, the game would satisfy every request and the player would know exactly where the piece is at any time. But the playfield is not empty. Since the spawn signal occurs after a piece locks, it could be abused to extract data out of the playfield, violating the concept of the blindfolded player. This is achieved by semihard dropping a piece into a column and observing how long it takes to lock, which is proportional to the height of the column. The entire surface of the pile could be probed.

To render such abuse ineffective, the method employs a version of Tetris that will not lock a piece until the player hard drops. The piece in play may rest indefinitely on a solid surface pending that explicit request to commit it to the pile.

Type Filtering

Building a structure logically-equivalent to a Boolean circuit requires placement of a specific sequence of Tetriminos. But the player cannot summon the pieces it needs into existence. Instead, it filters the provided pieces. When the randomizer spawns a piece that the player does not want, the player hard drops it onto an unbounded pile of discards in column −2.

Structures are made from the remaining pieces in columns ≥ 0. That infinite region—bounded on the bottom by the floor and on the left by the discards pile—serves as the workspace for building circuits.

Boolean Circuit Nodes

Nodes consist of pairs of vertically-adjacent cells. They can be in the following states.

  • 0 (false) — lower cell is solid, upper cell is empty.
  • 1 (true) — upper cell is solid, lower cell is empty or solid.
  • ? (unassigned) — both cells are empty.

Nodes are composed of one or more pairs, all in the same state. Typically, a node is a contiguous, horizontal line of pairs, but not necessarily. The pairs of a node can be separated in space, even vertically separated. The image below shows one possible encoding of 01010.

TetrisScript

The player builds structures by executing TetrisScript, a language that specifies where to put the pieces. It only has 3 instructions. The first consists of a mnemonic and a column index:

It means:

  1. Discard Tetriminos until the randomizer spawns the one specified by the mnemonic type.
  2. Rotate the piece into the mnemonic orientation.
  3. Shift the piece to column x.
  4. Hard drop the piece to lock it into the pile.

The second instruction type consists of a mnemonic, a column index, a row index, and a second column index:

mnemonic x1 y x2

It means:

  1. Discard Tetriminos until the randomizer spawns the one specified by the mnemonic type.
  2. Rotate the piece into the mnemonic orientation.
  3. Shift the piece to column x1.
  4. Semihard drop the piece to row y.
  5. Shift the piece to column x2.
  6. Hard drop the piece to lock it into the pile.

If a gravity drop were to happen after step 4, but before step 5 finishes, it would corrupt the structure. To prevent that possibility, the player maintains a timer synchronized with the fall timer. The player uses it to semihard drop immediately after a gravity drop, maximizing the time to perform the shift to column x2. The horizontal distance between x1 and x2 is always tiny, guaranteeing the shift can occur in the period between gravity drops.

Inverter (NOT)

An inverter is a single-input gate where the output is the complement of the input.

The code below makes an inverter by sandwiching an O-Tetrimino between horizontal L-and-J-Tetriminos.

not.t
lu 0
o 0 4 1
jd 0

in i -1..1 0
out o -1..1 5

Note: Although workspace coordinates are always ≥ 0 due to the floor and the discards pile, structure definitions can use negative column values because the coordinates are relative to where the structure is built.

The follow animation shows what happens for both input values.

When the input is 0, the L-Tetrimino lands low, which enables the O-Tetrimino to shift upon its protuberance. In turn, the J-Tetrimino lands high.

When the input is 1, the L-Tetrimino lands high and acts a barrier that obstructs the horizontal movement of the O-Tetrimino. The result is a configuration where the J-Tetrimino lands low.

In both cases, the output is the opposite of the input.

XOR

XOR is a 2-input gate that performs exclusive disjunction. It outputs 1 only when both inputs are unequal.

A ⊕ B

= (A ≠ B)

= AB + AB

= AA + AB + AB + BB

= A(A + B) + B(A + B)

= A C + B C, C = A + B

= A + C + B + C, C = A + B

Crossing

The wires of a circuit can cross over each other without connecting in a 3-dimensional universe. But, on the 2-dimensional playfield, a cross requires a planar device that mutually exchanges 2 inputs. The device implements the XOR swap algorithm. It stores the XOR of inputs A and B into a temporary variable, C:

C = A ⊕ B

Then it XORs the inputs with the temporary variable, which swaps them:

A ⊕ C = A ⊕ A ⊕ B = B

B ⊕ C = B ⊕ A ⊕ B = A

Functions

This section describes the arithmetic and logic functions created for the computer.

Interoperability

The functions are stackable. Meaning, the output of any function can serve as the input of another. To make that work:

  • Each function operates on an 8-bit, 16-bit, or 24-bit value.
  • The number of input bits always equals the number of output bits.
  • Each input node has a corresponding output node in the same column.
  • The terminals are evenly spaced, 10 cells apart. That width provides room for wide components, such as the swap circuit. And a round, base-10 number makes measurements easier.

Byte Match

The byte match function operates on a 2-byte array:

f( [A, X] ) = [ A, M ]

Memory

This section describes how memory works.

Implementation

Memory is an information storage device consisting of a rectangular pile with finite dimensions. The left edge of the pile abuts the left boundary of the workspace. The memory state is encoded as a sequence of bits in the top-two rows. The bits are regularly-spaced, 10 columns apart. And the number of bits is divisible by 8, which makes the sequence a finite list of bytes ordered left-to-right.

The bytes are manipulated by applying functions. This involves constructing a function on top of the bytes to be changed and using identity functions to keep the remaining bytes the same. Since functions have a uniform height, the identity functions also keep the pile rectangular.

Initialization

Although the player could initialize memory by dropping a sequence of constant functions directly onto the playfield floor, that would violate its principles. The blindfolded player can never be made aware of any memory state, including the initial state. Instead, the playfield, rather than starting out empty, begins with the input dataset already encoded within it. And, due to the way memory expands, the unallocated region is effectively initialized to zeros.

Dynamic Array Abstraction

When a function is constructed, it interacts exclusively with the surface of the pile. Everything beneath—the full history of memory—is irrelevant. Only the current state matters. This suggests an abstraction where memory operates like a dynamic array, a random access, variable-size list of bytes. Applying a function at byte i means updating bytes i through i + n, where n is the byte-width of the function. All the other bytes remain the same. In other words, even though the pile is a 2-dimensional matrix, it acts like a 1-dimensional array where functions modify element sequences.

Turing Machine

A Turing machine is a mathematical model used to investigate computability. There are several ways to define it. For this discussion, it consists of the following parts.

  • A tape drive.
    • Mounted onto the drive is an infinitely long tape capable of storing an endless a sequence of bits. A finite region of the tape is initialized with an input dataset. The remainder contains zeros.
    • The read/write head of the drive is limited to accessing a single bit. The drive can shift the tape to move the head one bit left or one bit right in the sequence. The head is initially positioned over the leftmost bit of the input dataset.
  • A state machine.
    • A register stores the current state, one of a finite many. It is initialized to a starting state.
    • A state-transition table with a finite number of entries. The key of each entry is the pair consisting of the current state and the current bit under the head. The value of each entry is a list of 3 instructions:
      1. Write a specified bit value at the position of the head.
      2. Move the head one bit in a specified direction.
      3. Update the register with a specified state.

During each cycle of the machine:

  1. The head reads a bit value.
  2. The bit value along with the current state is used to do a lookup in the state-transition table.
  3. The instructions in the looked-up entry are executed.

There are several ways to emulate a Turing machine using the dynamic array abstraction discussed above. The startup configuration of one approach places the state register into the first N bytes of the array, a0‥aN−1. N is large enough to cover all possible state values and the register is initialized with the starting state.

The byte immediately after the register, aN, is set to FF. It operates as a boundary marker.

The remaining bytes, aN+1‥a∞, represent the tape. The tape extends infinitely in both directions, but the array only does so in one. To compensate, the array stores 2 tracks as if the tape had been folded in half. That is:

  • Bit-0 of each byte, a:0i+N+1, stores the ith bit of the tape indexed from the leftmost bit of the input dataset and moving rightward.
  • Bit-1 of each byte, a:1i+N+1, stores the ith bit of the tape indexed from the bit left of leftmost bit of the input dataset and moving leftward.

The next two bits store the position of the head. Specifically:

  • Bit-2 of each byte, a:2i+N+1, is 1 iff the head is located at the ith bit of the tape indexed from the leftmost bit of the input dataset and moving rightward.
  • Bit-3 of each byte, a:3i+N+1, is 1 iff the head is located at the ith bit of the tape indexed from the bit left of leftmost bit of the input dataset and moving leftward.

If the head is not found (a:2i+N+1 and a:3i+N+1 are both 0), then T lets the inputs pass through, unchanged. Otherwise, T uses the data bit corresponding to the head, a:0i+N+1 or a:1i+N+1, in conjunction with the register value to update the data bit, to move the head, and to update the register value.

T is able to move the head because the 3 extra bytes include both adjacent neighbors of the byte containing the head. However, the first of the 3, ai+N, might be set to the boundary marker, FF, which represents the “fold” in the tape. This leads to the possibilities depicted in the following table.

Each triplet portrays a state of the 3 extra bytes. The center column contains the initial configurations. The left and right columns show the 8 possible outcomes after the head moves left and right, respectively. The upper and lower circles correspond to bits 2 and 3 of each byte, where an unfilled circle is a 0 and a filled circle is a 1.

In the first row, the head moves in the direction corresponding to the specified direction. In the second row, the head moves opposite to the specified direction because the tape is “folded over”. In the third and fourth rows, both circles of the left byte are filled since it contains the boundary marker. In the third row, moving left transfers the head from bit-2 to bit-3. Similarly, in the fourth row, moving right does the opposite.

A machine cycle consists of sliding the register from index 0 to a finite index k and then back to index 0, applying the transition function at each step along the way. k is initialized to 1 and it is incremented by 1 at the end of each cycle.

Since the head starts at the element corresponding to the leftmost bit of the input dataset and the transition function advances the head by at most one element, the expanding motion of the register is guaranteed to update the head at least once per cycle, and potentially multiple times per cycle. In this way, the machine always makes progress.

Each cycle of the machine requires more steps than the preceding cycle. This translates to an expanding, yet finite, number of TetrisScript instructions to execute per cycle, where k is encoded in increasing x-coordinate values.

The number cycles needed to complete the computation (for the machine to halt) is unknown in the general case. This necessitates an endless TetrisScript. However, since each cycle is an extension of its predecessor, the TetrisScript fed to the player could be synthesized on demand.

Tetris Machine

Like the Turing machine implementation described above, the machine that runs Tetris consists of a state register traversing an array. On startup, a program written in machine code—instructions and data encoded into a sequence of bytes of length L—is loaded into the array, followed by 2 padding bytes both set to zero, followed by a 21-byte state register, as shown below.

The machine code bytes operate as RAM with an address space of 0 to L−1. In the initial configuration above, the RAM addresses of the elements equal the array indices. However, as the state registers moves, elements shift position. Those below the state register retain RAM addresses equal to the array indices while those above it acquire RAM addresses equal to the indices minus the width of the state register (address = index − 21).

At runtime, the length of the array remains constant. The RAM filled with machine code constitutes the full extent of the machine’s memory. This constraint makes the machine a deterministic linear bounded automata, where “linear bounded” means the memory capacity is fixed at a linear function of the input size, L. It is not as powerful as a Turing machine. But, without a dependency on infinite storage, the machine is physically realizable.

The transition function operates on a 25-element subarray that includes the 3 bytes immediately below the state register and the byte immediately above it. As revealed in the following diagram, those bytes are called I, J, K, and Q, respectively, regardless of the state register’s location.

Several of the bytes represent segments of 16-bit or 24-bit words:

In the center is the 16-bit address register, a. At startup, it is initialized to the highest RAM address, L−1. That value corresponds to the element at the state register’s start index minus 3. When the state register takes a downward step, a is decremented by 1. Likewise, when the state register takes an upward step, a is incremented by 1. This keeps a pointed at element I:

Since a is a 16-bit register, the maximum RAM size, Lmax, is 216 B = 64 KiB.

During each cycle of the machine, the state register slides from one end of the array to the other. On even cycles, it descends from index L+2 to index 3 (a decrements from address L−1 to address 0). And on odd cycles, it ascends from index 3 to index L+2 (a increments from address 0 to address L−1). In other words, on each cycle, the state register traverses all of RAM.

In the course of the state register traversal, a machine code instruction is fetched from RAM into the instruction register, ijk. Instructions vary in length from 1 to 3 bytes, where the first byte is the opcode and the rest, if any, is an 8-bit or a 16-bit operand. At 24 bits wide, ijk has the capacity to store any instruction.

The diagram below shows ijk along with the 16-bit program counter, P, which holds the address of the instruction to be fetched. As the state register slides across RAM, IJK is copied to ijk at the moment P equals a.

After the slide, the instruction in ijk is decoded and executed. If the instruction does not take up the full width of ijk, the remainder is discarded.

After P is incremented, the fetched instruction is decoded and executed. The process begins with the transfer instructions. Each copies a specified 8-bit source register to a specified 8-bit destination register. The register choices are:

  • The accumulator, A, which operates as both an input and the output of arithmetic and logic instructions.
  • The data register, B, which operates as an input of arithmetic and logic instructions.
  • The high memory address register, M, which is the upper byte of the 16-bit memory address register, MN. MN is used as the source address of load instructions and the destination address of store instructions.
  • The low memory address register, N, which is the lower byte of MN.

Because Tetris is a game, it is designed to loop forever. The machine never halts. This necessitates an endless stream of TetrisScript fed to the player. The stream is synthesized on demand by perpetually alternating between expansions of the downward and upward cycle MetaTetrisScript programs.

A label is a placeholder for an address. In this case, it is the address of the code to run if the branch is taken.

A label is declared immediately before target code or data, enabling resolution of its address during assembly. The declaration consists of a unique name followed by a colon, as shown below.

name:

Here are all the branch instructions:

InstructionNamePseudocodeOpcode
JMP labelJumpP = label;20
BNE labelBranch Not Equalif (z == 0) P = label;22
BEQ labelBranch Equalif (z == 1) P = label;23
BPL labelBranch Plusif (n == 0) P = label;24
BMI labelBranch Minusif (n == 1) P = label;25
JSR labelJump SubroutineR = P; P = label;28
RTSReturn SubroutineP = R;70

RTS is a 1-byte instruction. The rest are 3-byte instructions consisting of an opcode followed by the label address encoded big-endian. The opcode is composed of bits 0010rstu:

  • If t is 1, then the zero flag, z, must equal u to branch.
  • If s is 1, then the negative flag, n, must equal u to branch.
  • If r is 1, then the program counter, P, is copied to the return register, R, before jumping. That feature is used by JSR while RTS does the opposite. This enables subroutine calls one level deep. No other instructions access R.

The conditional branch instructions—BNE, BEQ, BPL, and BMI—can serve as relational operators by branching on the result of A - B. However, BPL and BMI treat the difference as a signed number. This leads to the following limitations.

InstructionRelationLimitations
BNEA ≠ BA - B ≠ 0
BEQA = BA - B = 0
BPLA ≥ BA - B ∊ [0, 127]
BMIA < BA - B ∊ [-128, -1]

Since there is no carry flag nor its complementary BCC and BCS instructions, an inequality test for an unsigned difference requires multiple steps. One option is to logical right shift A and B by 1 before subtracting. If the difference is nonzero, then the test is complete. Otherwise, the original bit-0s need to be compared.

While there is no indirect branch instruction, self-modifying code can be used to alter a branch target at runtime.

By default, program execution starts with all registers initialized to 0. Since that includes the P register, the default entry point is the first line of code. However, if the program includes a label named main, then P is set to its address at startup, as if a jump automatically occurred to that location.

Load Instructions

A load reads the memory value at the address in MN into either A or B:

InstructionNamePseudocodeOpcode
LDALoad AA = *MN;40
LDBLoad BB = *MN;41

The zero flag, z, is set to 1 if the read value is zero; otherwise, it is reset to 0.

The negative flag, n, is set to 1 if the read value is negative (if bit-7 is 1); otherwise, it is reset to 0.

Each load instruction maps to an opcode with bits 0100000d, where A and B correspond to d values 0 and 1, respectively.

Store Instructions

A store instruction writes the value in either A or B to memory at the address in MN:

InstructionNamePseudocodeOpcode
STAStore A*MN = A;30
STBStore B*MN = B;31

No flags are affected.

Each store instruction maps to an opcode with bits 0011000s, where A and B correspond to s values 0 and 1, respectively.

Set Instructions

A set instruction moves an immediate (a constant) into a register:

InstructionNamePseudocodeOpcode
SEA immediateSet AA = immediate;50
SEB immediateSet BB = immediate;51
SMN immediateSet MNMN = immediate;2F

SEA and SEB accept a byte immediate whereas SMN accepts a 16-bit word immediate.

The instructions assemble to an opcode followed by the immediate byte or bytes encoded big-endian.

The immediate can be an integer literal written in hexadecimal. As shown below, a byte literal contains 2 hex digits while a word literal contains 4.

SEA D3                  ; A = 0xD3;
SEB 7F                  ; B = 0x7F;
SMN 1234                ; MN = 0x1234;

The define directive equates a name with an integer literal:

define name value

Defined values can serve as immediate operands:

define X D3             ; #define X 0xD3
define Y 7F             ; #define Y 0x7F
define Z 1234           ; #define Z 0x1234

SEA X                   ; A = X;
SEB Y                   ; B = Y;
SMN Z                   ; MN = Z;

To reserve and initialize a data region, an integer literal or a sequence of integer literals is written without a preceding instruction mnemonic. Accessing the region requires its address. One way to get it is to preface the region with a label. And that label can successively be used as immediate operand. This is demonstrated in the following example where the declaration of the label v is followed by a byte literal.

v: 00                   ; v = 0;

SMN v                   ; MN = &v;
LDA                     ; A = *MN;
INC                     ; ++A;
STA                     ; *MN = A;

v is a placeholder for the address of the single-byte data region. But, since it can be used as an operand, it works like a byte variable. Lines 3–6 are equivalent to ++v;

The next example reserves and initializes an 8-element byte array, table, and a byte variable, index.

segment 0180

table:                  ; table[] = { 0x01, 0x02, 0x04, 0x08, 0x10, 0x20, 0x40, 0x80 }; 
01 02 04 08
10 20 40 80

index: 03               ; index = 0x03;

SMN index               ; MN = &index;
LDA                     ; A = *MN;
SMN table               ; MN = table;
TNB                     ; B = N;
ADD                     ; A += B;
TAN                     ; N = A;
LDA                     ; A = *MN;

Lines 9–15 are equivalent to:

A = table[index];

That can be expressed as:

A = *(table + index);

However, without a carry flag, adding a 16-bit value to an 8-bit value is challenging. Instead, the code takes advantage of the segment directive, which has the following format.

segment address

It instructs the assembler to store bytes generated from subsequent code at the specified address. In this case, the effect is:

table = 0x0180;

Since the table is fully contained within the same 256-byte memory page, adding table and index does not produce a carry. The code offsets only the low-byte of the address.

To facilitate self-modifying code, a label, when used as an operand, can be offset by a positive or negative constant with the notation below.

label[+-]offset

The offset is a fixed integer value written in decimal.

The notation provides a way to modify the operand of an instruction, as seen in the following example.

SEA FF                  ; A = 0xFF;
SMN target+1            ; MN = target + 1;
STA                     ; *MN = A;

target:
SEB 00                  ; B = 0x00; // until replaced with 0xFF

target points to the opcode of the SEB instruction. At runtime, lines 1–3 change the immediate operand, which is located one byte past the opcode, from 00 to FF.

In summary, the operand of SMN can be any of the following.

SMN word | label | label+offset | label-offset

The same applies to branch instructions; i.e., the target of the branch can any of the above. But it is rarely worthwhile to jump to a numerical address or to a label offset by a constant.

Tetris Program

The Tetris program is a barebones implementation of the game that employs the right-handed Nintendo Rotation System (NRS). As a randomly-selected Tetrimino falls, the player shifts it left or right, rotates it clockwise or counterclockwise, or soft drops it. When the piece locks into the pile, completed lines are cleared, the rows above them shift downward, and the next piece spawns.

The game does not keep track of the number of lines cleared, nor the score or any other statistics. That being the case, the level never increases and the drop speed remains constant. There is no hard drop, no piece preview, no ghost piece, no hold piece, no music or sound effects, no multiplayer or garbage, no title screen, menus, configuration, demo, or endings. There is nothing beyond the core game mechanics.

The program begins by defining a set of constants:

FALL_SPEED is the initial value of the fall timer. On each frame, if the fall timer is not 0, then it is decremented by 1. Otherwise, a gravity drop occurs and the fall timer is reset to FALL_SPEED. A FALL_SPEED of 1 means a gravity drop occurs every other frame. Since the frame rate is approximately 5 frames/sec, a gravity drop happens about every 400 milliseconds. That fall speed is roughly equivalent to level 5 in NES Tetris.

The program switches between the modes defined on lines 3–5. MODE_ATTRACT is a very basic attract mode that simply keeps the last top out on display, or an empty playfield at startup. It is unresponsive to controls except for the Start button, which clears the playfield and transitions to MODE_PLAY. As its name suggests, that is the mode where the player interacts with the falling pieces. When a piece locks into the pile, the program temporarily changes to MODE_CLEAR_LINES, during which it scans for and removes completed lines.

The program declares a single subroutine called drawOrTestTetrimino that performs the actions defined on lines 7 and 8. ACTION_TEST directs drawOrTestTetrimino to verify that four playfield cells corresponding to the blocks of a named Tetrimino at a specified location and orientation are all empty. It enables the program to determine if a requested move is a valid move.

Since the language does not support a call stack, drawOrTestTetrimino receives the Tetrimino type, coordinates, and orientation via global variables, and the action and the color via self-modifying code. The action constants are the opcodes for BNE and BEQ. They are used to overwrite an instruction in drawOrTestTetrimino to alter its behavior.

M = 0;               // page zero

As discussed thus far, the machine that runs Tetris is comprised of an 8-bit CPU and up to 64 KiB of RAM initialized with the instructions and the data of the program to be executed. RAM initialization is carried out by a mechanism analogous to a peripheral data storage device. However, with that peripheral alone, the machine is limited to batch processing. To establish a real-time interface between the machine and a human player, additional peripherals are required.

The randomization function employs the same Fibonacci linear-feedback shift register (LFSR) at the core of the pseudorandom number generator (PRNG) in NES Tetris. As shown below, the next random bit, nextBit, is generated by XORing bit-1s of seedHigh and seedLow.

seedHigh and seedLow collectively operate as a 16-bit register. It is logical right shifted, during which the lowest bit is lost and the highest bit is set to nextBit.

The initial high and low seeds are 89 and 88, respectively, the same, arbitrary quantities used in NES Tetris.

The LFSR produces a sequence of 32,767 unique values before cycling, one less-than half the numbers that fit in a 16-bit register. To prevent that deterministic sequence from producing a constant Tetrimino order, the code bases its selection on seedHigh XORed with the frameCounter. As explained above, the frameCounter increments at the beginning of every frame, even during attract mode. This means, the order depends on the number of frames between program startup (or game over) and Start button activation. It is similarly affected by the number of frames between spawns. In other words, the code taps into the inherent randomness of the human player.

Because there are seven Tetriminos, the XORed value needs to be reduced to the range [ 0, 6 ]. That could be achieved with a modulo operation that produces the remainder of integer division by 7. But mod is computationally expensive. Instead, code based on the following expression is used, where v is the lower-five bits of the XORed value.

7 * v / 32

Since v ∈ [ 0, 31 ], 7 is multiplied by a fraction less-than 1.0, producing a value in the correct range.

The expression is transformed into a version that is both efficient and readily implementable thusly:

= (8 * v - v) / 32

= ((v << 3) - v) / 32

= ((v << 3) - v) >>> 5

If the randomly chosen Tetrimino is the same as the last one, the program picks again. That simple strategy does not prevent alternating duplicate pieces. Nor does it inhibit droughts.

The handler calls drawOrTestTetrimino to paint the falling Tetrimino with empty cells, erasing it from the playfield. The paint value, CELL_EMPTY, is written to drawCell+1, the address of an operand of an instruction in drawOrTestTetrimino. Meaning, as with drawOrTest, the write modifies code.

The handler backs up the orientation and the x-coordinate of the falling Tetrimino.

If the Left button is pressed, the handler decrements the x-coordinate by 1. Otherwise, if the Right button is pressed, the handler increments the x-coordinate by 1.

If the Counterclockwise Rotation button is pressed, the handler decrements the orientation by 1, such that 0 wraps to 3. Otherwise, if the Clockwise Rotation button is pressed, the handler increments the orientation by 1, such that 3 wraps to 0.

The handler verifies that the manipulated Tetrimino is fully within empty space by calling drawOrTestTetrimino. If it is not, the original orientation and x-coordinate are restored from the backups.

If the Down button is pressed, fallTimer is assigned zero.

If fallTimer is zero, the handler resets it to FALL_SPEED. Then, the handler drops the Tetrimino by incrementing its y-coordinate by 1. If the dropped Tetrimino is not fully within empty space, per a check by drawOrTestTetrimino, then the y-coordinate is decremented by 1, restoring its position to where it now locks into the pile. Subsequently, minY is updated based off the new pile height and the program switches to clear lines mode.

After that, the program flows into the code that normally runs when fallTimer is not zero. It decrements fallTimer by 1 and it calls drawOrTestTetrimino to draw the Tetrimino, which may now be at a different location and orientation from where it was erased at the start of the play handler. The paint value is tetriminoType + 1, because 0 represents an empty cell.

The handler ends by jumping back to the beginning of the program, where, if the Tetrimino locked into the pile, it will invoke the clear lines handler in the listing below.

The clear lines handler executes after a Tetrimino locks into the pile. It scans for and removes lines. The NRS centers the 7 Tetriminos at 19 different orientations in 5x5 matrices. But the bottom row of every matrix is completely empty. This means, only four rows need to be checked for lines in the range [ tetriminoY–2, tetriminoY+1 ]. The clear lines handler loops backwards over that range. It initializes global variables i to 3 and origin to the index of the first element of the tetriminoY + 1 row. At the end of each iteration, i is decremented by 1 and origin is decremented by PLAYFIELD_WIDTH, repositioning it to the start of the previous row. The loop terminates when i drops below zero. When a line is cleared, all the rows above it shift downward, a time-consuming process owing to the amount of data needed to be copied. To minimize the time, the empty rows above the pile are not shifted because empty space looks the same shifted or not. The first row above the pile guaranteed to be completed empty is derived from minY, the y-coordinate of the highest Tetrimino on the pile. It is row minY – 3, or 0, whichever is larger. In another example of self-modifying code, the handler assigns the byte at minN+1 to the index of the first element of that row.
